Journey to Scale

Turning ideas into impact for girls!

2017-10-23

“Innovation” seems to be the buzzword of the moment in the development and humanitarian sectors. It’s used in many contexts, including new financing mechanisms, new technologies, and new social norm models to support gender opportunities and the rights of girls.

In an ever-changing, ever-connected, complex world, the challenges and barriers girls and women are facing have evolved as well. To add on to persisting global issues like child marriage and lack of access to health services and secondary education, new issues have emerged as well. There has been a rise in obesity rates amongst women, linking to the increase in noncommunicable diseases. Girls and women representation in both Internet usership and the STEM workforce lag behind their boy and men counterparts. And in this digital and social media era, a new form of gender-based harassment has taken shape online.

To tackle both persisting and emerging issues girls and women face, new solutions are needed; hence, “innovation” is welcome. But although I’ve seen the term “innovation” being thrown around to give a sexy, fresh facelift to many projects and initiatives alike, I have yet to see a definition of innovation for gender equality that answers: What is a successful innovation?

Borrowing from best practices of forward-thinking-acting sectors with innovation at its core, from high-tech where startups operate on principles like “move fast, break things” and a culture of “hacking”, to finance where firms have an obsession with measuring return on investment, I’ve tailored these learnings to the context of the development sector and the efforts towards gender equality and girls’ empowerment.
